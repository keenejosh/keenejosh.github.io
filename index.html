Doctoral & Master's Work

Research

Doctoral Research · PAMP-Induced Tau Pathology & Neuroinflammation

We examined whether repeated peripheral immune challenges drive lasting tau pathology and neuroinflammation in PS19 tauopathy mice, with tissue collected 30 days post-challenge to capture sustained rather than acute effects.

Primary Finding

  • Repeated PAMP administration drove a 5.4-fold hippocampal p-tau (AT8) elevation at 30 days post-challenge, with total tau (HT7) unaltered, consistent with post-translational hyperphosphorylation rather than increased transgene expression.

Supporting Finding

  • A significant genotype × treatment interaction for MHC II and positive correlations between p-tau and glial reactivity markers (MHC II, GFAP, CD68) suggest peripheral immune activation and tau pathology converge to amplify neuroinflammatory responses.
Dissertation (PDF)

Master's Thesis · Prenatal Inflammatory Stress & Amyloid Burden

We examined whether prenatal inflammatory exposure shapes Alzheimer's-related pathology in 5xFAD offspring, comparing hippocampal and cortical amyloid burden and microglial density by maternal genotype.

Primary Finding

  • Prenatal inflammatory stress paradoxically reduced hippocampal amyloid burden by ~52% in offspring of transgenic mothers, with effects reaching significance independently in both male and female offspring.

Supporting Finding

  • Microglial density was unaffected by maternal genotype, suggesting the plaque reduction reflects altered amyloid processing rather than increased microglial clearance.
